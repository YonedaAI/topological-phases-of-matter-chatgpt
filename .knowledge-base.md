# Shared Knowledge Base: Condensed Topological Phases of Matter

## Purpose and claim discipline

This file is the common source of definitions, hypotheses, examples, and limits for five papers and a synthesis paper. The project studies whether condensed mathematics can organize families of local quantum systems without pretending that sheaf language solves the analytic or microscopic realization problems.

Every substantive claim in the papers must carry one of these labels:

- `ESTABLISHED`: a cited theorem or standard construction under stated hypotheses.
- `PROPOSED`: a new definition or framework introduced by this project.
- `CONJECTURAL`: a precise claim that is not proved here.
- `OPEN`: no proof or counterexample is known in the stated scope.
- `OBSTRUCTED`: a cited theorem or explicit counterexample rules out the claim as stated.

The central research sequence is:

\[
\text{local quantum interactions}
\longrightarrow
\text{condensed moduli stack of Hamiltonians}
\longrightarrow
\text{uniformly gapped substack}
\longrightarrow
\text{stabilized phase }\infty\text{-groupoid}
\longrightarrow
\text{invertible condensed phase spectrum}.
\]

Only the first four steps have close existing mathematical analogues in the cited literature. The final condensed spectrum and the comparison maps joining the full sequence are `PROPOSED`.

## Shared conventions

### Spatial system

Fix a uniformly locally finite metric space \((\Gamma,d)\), usually a lattice such as \(\mathbb Z^d\). Each site \(x\in\Gamma\) carries a finite-dimensional Hilbert space \(\mathcal H_x\). For finite \(\Lambda\subset\Gamma\), set

\[
\mathcal A_\Lambda=\bigotimes_{x\in\Lambda}\mathcal B(\mathcal H_x).
\]

The quasi-local observable algebra is the C*-inductive limit

\[
\mathcal A_\Gamma=overline{\bigcup_{\Lambda\Subset\Gamma}\mathcal A_\Lambda},
\]

using the compatible isometric inclusions \(A\mapsto A\otimes 1\). This is an `ESTABLISHED` construction.

Spatial dimension is denoted by \(d\). Spacetime dimension is denoted by \(n=d+1\). Freed-Hopkins tables use \(n\), so the papers must never silently identify the two.

### Interactions and locality

An interaction is a map

\[
\Phi:\{X\Subset\Gamma\}\longrightarrow \mathcal A_X^{\mathrm{sa}}.
\]

Choose a nonincreasing function \(F:[0,\infty)\to(0,\infty)\) that is uniformly summable and has finite convolution constant:

\[
\sup_x\sum_y F(d(x,y))<\infty,
\qquad
\sup_{x,y}\frac{1}{F(d(x,y))}
\sum_z F(d(x,z))F(d(z,y))<\infty.
\]

For a general countable metric space, thermodynamic-limit arguments should also state the uniform tail condition

\[
\sup_x\sum_{d(x,y)\geq R}F(d(x,y))\longrightarrow0
\qquad(R\to\infty).
\]

It is automatic for the standard translation-invariant lattice examples, but it should not be inferred from pointwise summability without checking the geometry.

One standard interaction norm is

\[
\|\Phi\|_F
=
\sup_{x,y\in\Gamma}
\frac{1}{F(d(x,y))}
\sum_{X\ni x,y}\|\Phi(X)\|.
\]

For a parameter family \(s\mapsto\Phi_s\), distinguish two requirements:

1. Uniform locality: \(\sup_s\|\Phi_s\|_F<\infty\). This yields Lieb-Robinson constants uniform in the parameter and finite volume.
2. Local parameter continuity: the coefficients relevant to each local observable vary continuously, with uniform tail control. This can yield point-norm continuity of \(s\mapsto\tau_t^{(s)}(A)\) for each local \(A\).

Global continuity into the interaction Banach norm implies both conditions on compact parameter spaces, but it can be too strong for product-topology disorder. Compactness plus coefficientwise continuity does not imply a uniform interaction-norm bound.

The shared Lieb-Robinson estimate has the schematic form

\[
\|[\tau_t(A),B]\|
\leq C\|A\|\|B\|
\sum_{x\in X,\,y\in Y}F(d(x,y))
\bigl(e^{v|t|}-1\bigr),
\]

for \(A\in\mathcal A_X\), \(B\in\mathcal A_Y\), with \(C\) and \(v\) determined by the geometry and a uniform bound on \(\|\Phi\|_F\). This statement is `ESTABLISHED` under the detailed hypotheses in the cited Lieb-Robinson literature.

### Condensed parameter objects

A condensed set is a sheaf of sets on profinite sets for finite jointly surjective covers. For a topological space \(X\), the associated presheaf is

\[
\underline X(S)=\operatorname{Cont}(S,X).
\]

Full faithfulness requires the compact-generation and cardinal conventions stated by Clausen and Scholze. The papers must not claim unqualified full faithfulness for every topological space.

A condensed anima is an appropriate hypercomplete anima-valued sheaf. It can encode automorphisms and higher equivalences. Not every condensed anima is represented by a topological space.

For a compact Hausdorff space \(S\) and a C*-algebra \(A\),

\[
C(S,A)
\]

is a C*-algebra with pointwise operations, pointwise involution, and

\[
\|f\|_\infty=\sup_{s\in S}\|f(s)\|_A.
\]

Positivity is fiberwise: \(f\geq0\) if and only if \(f(s)\geq0\) for every \(s\).

Do not identify \(\operatorname{State}(C(S,A))\) with \(C(S,\operatorname{State}(A))\). The latter is a continuous family of fiber states. The former also includes measure and disintegration data over \(S\).

For profinite \(S\) and Banach \(A\), locally constant functions that factor through finite clopen quotients are sup-norm dense in \(C(S,A)\). This analytic density statement supports finite-resolution approximation. The sheaf condition alone does not prove that every invariant is determined by finite quotients.

For a finite jointly surjective family \(S_i\to S\) of profinite spaces, the canonical map

\[
C(S,A)
\longrightarrow
\operatorname{Eq}\left(
\prod_iC(S_i,A)
\rightrightarrows
\prod_{i,j}C(S_i\times_SS_j,A)
\right)
\]

is an isomorphism of C*-algebras. The gluing map is continuous because the finite disjoint-union cover is a quotient map between compact Hausdorff spaces. The norm and positivity descend because the cover is jointly surjective.

### Disorder

If \(D\) is finite, then \(\Omega=D^{\mathbb Z^d}\) is compact and profinite. With the shift action \(\alpha:\mathbb Z^d\curvearrowright\Omega\), a standard covariant bulk algebra is

\[
C(\Omega)\rtimes_\alpha\mathbb Z^d,
\]

possibly twisted by a magnetic cocycle. The condensed framework naturally accepts \(\Omega\) as a test object, but all locality, gap, covariance, and representation hypotheses remain separate analytic input.

## Paper 1: Locality and parameterized Lieb-Robinson estimates

### Target theorem

`PROPOSED`: construct a condensed moduli functor of interactions whose \(S\)-points satisfy uniform locality and local parameter continuity, then prove that thermodynamic dynamics and Lieb-Robinson estimates are natural in \(S\).

A defensible theorem should assume:

- uniformly locally finite metric geometry;
- finite-dimensional on-site Hilbert spaces, or the additional domain hypotheses required for unbounded on-site terms;
- an admissible \(F\)-function;
- \(\sup_{s\in S}\|\Phi_s\|_F<\infty\);
- local parameter continuity sufficient for point-norm continuity on local observables.

The conclusion should be limited to existence of thermodynamic dynamics, uniform propagation estimates, and parameter continuity. It must not claim a spectral gap.

### Counterexample separating locality from a uniform gap

Let \(S=\mathbb N\cup\{\infty\}\) be the one-point compactification. Put an on-site term of strength \(1/n\) at a site that moves to infinity as \(n\to\infty\), and let the limiting interaction omit that defect. Coefficients converge locally and every fiber can be gapped, but the gaps along finite \(n\) can collapse like \(1/n\). Thus local continuity plus pointwise gappedness does not imply a uniform gap.

### Dependencies

- Supplies the topology on interaction families used by Papers 3, 4, and 5.
- Supplies quasi-local dynamics needed to define adiabatic equivalence.
- Does not depend on K-theory or bordism.

### Nonclaims

- A profinite parameter does not automatically make Lieb-Robinson constants uniform.
- A Lieb-Robinson bound does not imply a thermodynamic spectral gap.
- Coefficientwise continuity does not automatically define continuity in a global interaction norm.

## Paper 2: Positivity, C*-norms, and solid K-theory

### Target result

`PROPOSED`: define the observable-algebra component of the condensed Hamiltonian stack using C*-algebra objects and positive morphisms, and prove exact pointwise C*-algebra statements for compact or profinite parameter families.

The minimal reliable bridge is:

\[
\Phi_s
\longmapsto
\mathcal A_\Gamma
\longmapsto
C(S,\mathcal A_\Gamma)
\longmapsto
K^{\mathrm{top}}(C(S,\mathcal A_\Gamma)).
\]

This does not reconstruct the interaction from the K-class.

The algebraic involution does not select a physical C*-completion. For example, give \(\mathbb C[x]\) the involution \(x^*=x\). Each compact infinite \(K\subset\mathbb R\) gives a faithful pre-C*-norm

\[
\|p\|_K=\sup_{t\in K}|p(t)|.
\]

The choices \(K=[-1,1]\) and \(K=[-2,2]\) give different norms and positive cones. Hence condensed algebraic descent cannot manufacture the C*-norm or positivity required by physics.

The infinite-volume Hamiltonian is generally not a bounded element of \(\mathcal A_\Gamma\). Under locality hypotheses, an interaction determines finite-volume Hamiltonians and thermodynamic dynamics or a densely defined derivation. Paper 2 must not write a formal infinite sum as though it were automatically a quasi-local observable.

### Aoki's theorem and its exact scope

`ESTABLISHED`: Aoki proves that solidification of algebraic K-theory recovers connective topological K-theory for real and complex Banach algebras, with the stated connective and Bott-periodic qualifications. In the complex case, the formulation includes

\[
\bigl(K^{\mathrm{cn}}(A)\bigr)^{\mathrm{sol}}
\simeq \tau_{\geq0}K^{\mathrm{top}}(A),
\]

and Bott inversion recovers periodic topological K-theory. The real theory uses the degree-eight real Bott element.

This is a K-theory-level comparison. It is not:

- a reconstruction of the C*-norm;
- a reconstruction of the positive cone or state space;
- a proof that every algebraic K-class is a physical phase;
- an equivalence between microscopic Hamiltonians and their observable algebras.

For a norm-continuous path of self-adjoint elements \(h_t\in M_n(B)\) with a common spectral gap about zero, functional calculus gives a norm-continuous path of Fermi projections

\[
p_t=\chi_{(-\infty,0)}(h_t).
\]

Thus \([p_t]\in K_0(B)\) is constant. This is the controlled microscopic-to-K-theory map used by Paper 4. Real, graded, and odd symmetry classes require the corresponding real, graded, or \(K_1\) construction.

### Dependencies

- Uses Paper 1 for parameterized interactions and dynamics.
- Supplies operator-algebraic targets to Papers 4 and 5.
- Supplies the precise condensed-to-operator K-theory bridge used in the synthesis.

## Paper 3: Thermodynamic gaps and stability

### Gap definitions

For finite \(\Lambda\), let \(H_\Lambda\) be the local Hamiltonian. If \(E_0(\Lambda)\) is its ground energy and the ground-state sector is allowed to be degenerate, define the finite-volume gap as the distance from that sector to the rest of the spectrum.

A uniform finite-volume gap requires a constant \(\gamma>0\), independent of sufficiently large volume and compatible boundary conditions, such that

\[
\operatorname{gap}(H_\Lambda)\geq\gamma.
\]

An infinite-volume GNS gap is a spectral statement about the positive generator of the dynamics in a ground-state representation. The two notions are related only under stated limiting and boundary hypotheses. They must not be treated as synonyms.

When finite-volume degeneracy can split by exponentially small amounts, use a selected ground-band interval \(I_\Lambda\) and define

\[
\gamma_{\mathrm{band}}(\Lambda)
=
\operatorname{dist}\bigl(
\operatorname{spec}(H_\Lambda)\cap I_\Lambda,
\operatorname{spec}(H_\Lambda)\setminus I_\Lambda
\bigr).
\]

This is the gap notion used by spectral flow. The literal ground-state gap and the ground-band gap can differ when the dimension of the lowest spectral cluster changes.

For a selected infinite-volume ground state \(\omega\), let \((\mathcal H_\omega,\pi_\omega,\Omega_\omega)\) be its GNS representation and let \(H_\omega\geq0\) implement the dynamics. Define

\[
\gamma_{\mathrm{GNS}}(\omega)
=
\sup\{\gamma>0:(0,\gamma)\cap\operatorname{spec}(H_\omega)=\varnothing\}.
\]

The selected state is part of the datum. An interaction can admit inequivalent ground-state representations with different spectral questions.

### Gap counterexamples

For the compact parameter space \(S=\{0\}\cup\{1/n:n\geq2\}\), consider

\[
H_s=\operatorname{diag}(0,s,1).
\]

The matrix family is norm-continuous and every fiber has a positive literal ground-state gap, but the infimum of those gaps is zero. The ground-state multiplicity changes at \(s=0\). If the two lowest levels are instead treated as one band, the band gap stays positive. The convention is mathematically substantive.

As a thermodynamic example, every finite open isotropic ferromagnetic spin chain is gapped, but a one-magnon excitation has energy bounded by

\[
1-\cos(\pi/L)\sim\frac{\pi^2}{2L^2}.
\]

The gap therefore closes as \(L\to\infty\). Finite-size positivity alone proves no thermodynamic gap.

### Established stability lane

`ESTABLISHED`: uniformly gapped paths satisfying the hypotheses of quasi-adiabatic continuation yield spectral-flow automorphisms of the quasi-local algebra. Bachmann, Michalakis, Nachtergaele, and Sims prove automorphic equivalence within a gapped phase under precise regularity, locality, and gap conditions.

`ESTABLISHED`: Michalakis and Pytel prove perturbative stability for frustration-free Hamiltonians under Local Topological Quantum Order and additional locality hypotheses. This is not a theorem for arbitrary gapped Hamiltonians.

A controlled theorem in the later Nachtergaele-Sims-Young formulation assumes a positive pre-existing bulk GNS gap, frustration freeness, finite-range positive unperturbed terms, local gap control, LTQO with quantitative decay, regular geometry, and a stretched-exponentially decaying anchored perturbation. Under these hypotheses, sufficiently small perturbations preserve a prescribed smaller bulk gap and transport the ground state by a quasi-local automorphism. Every hypothesis must remain visible in Paper 3.

### Proposed condensed gap subfunctor

For a parameter object \(S\), define an \(S\)-family to be uniformly gapped only if a single \(\gamma>0\) works for all \(s\in S\), all sufficiently large volumes, and the specified boundary class. `PROPOSED`: prove descent and homotopy invariance for this restricted functor.

### Nonclaims

- Pointwise gaps over a compact or profinite parameter space need not have a positive infimum.
- Gap stability requires more than locality and small perturbation norm in known interacting theorems.
- Spectral flow preserves phase data along a path already known to be uniformly gapped. It does not prove that the path is gapped.

## Paper 4: Microscopic lattice systems and effective field theories

### Three distinct arrows

The paper must keep these maps separate:

\[
\text{microscopic Hamiltonian}
\longrightarrow
\text{low-energy effective theory},
\]

\[
\text{low-energy theory}
\longrightarrow
\text{deformation or homotopy class},
\]

\[
\text{abstract class}
\longrightarrow
\text{explicit microscopic representative}.
\]

No cited theorem identifies all three arrows for general interacting systems.

### Free-fermion controlled sector

For a bounded self-adjoint one-particle Hamiltonian \(H\) with \(0\notin\operatorname{spec}(H)\), spectral flattening gives

\[
Q=\operatorname{sgn}(H),
\qquad Q^*=Q,
\qquad Q^2=1.
\]

The flattening homotopy must remain gapped and respect the required symmetries. In the free-fermion setting, Kitaev and Thiang connect symmetry classes and stabilized obstruction classes to operator K-theory. Thiang stresses that K-theory is naturally relative to a reference phase, not an absolute classification of every microscopic system.

The controlled classification fixes a quadratic system, symmetry class, base algebra, norm-continuous gapped homotopy, and stabilization convention. It retains the stable occupied or empty grading, symmetry-compatible obstruction class, and stacking law. It generally forgets the gap magnitude, dispersion, correlation length, locality constants, microscopic couplings, finite-band unstable data, and interactions outside the quadratic sector.

Fidkowski and Kitaev provide a decisive boundary: the free one-dimensional BDI classification is \(\mathbb Z\), while symmetry-preserving interactions reduce it to \(\mathbb Z_8\). Therefore free K-theory cannot be promoted to a universal interacting classification.

### EFT scope

An effective field theory forgets ultraviolet lattice data. A comparison theorem must specify at least:

- which microscopic category and equivalences are used;
- which scaling or low-energy limit exists;
- which symmetries and anomalies survive;
- whether boundaries and defects are included;
- whether the map is injective, surjective, or only invariant-producing.

`OPEN`: a general equivalence between the proposed condensed microscopic stack and an effective-field-theory stack.

A serious comparison table must record separate verdicts for construction, invariance under gapped homotopy, faithfulness, fullness, essential surjectivity, compatibility with stacking, symmetry preservation, and treatment of boundaries or defects. A map that only produces an invariant is not an equivalence.

### Defects and transition classes

For free Bloch or BdG families \(H(k,r)\), a sphere in the parameter \(r\) can link a defect or gapless locus. Teo and Kane classify such stable free-fermion defect data in the controlled symmetry setting. This is a physical precedent for a relative transition class, not a theorem for arbitrary interacting condensed families.

For the SSH model near \(t_1=t_2\) and \(k=\pi\), write \(m=t_1-t_2\) and \(k=\pi+p\). Then

\[
q(k)\approx m-it_2p.
\]

On a small circle linking \((p,m)=(0,0)\), the normalized map to \(S^1\) has degree \(\pm1\), equal to the jump of the AIII winding number. The combined momentum-parameter disk is essential. Breaking chiral symmetry permits a gapped detour, so the charge is relative to the fixed AIII symmetry problem.

### Nonclaims

- Spectral flattening is not available as stated for arbitrary interacting many-body Hamiltonians.
- Equality of K-theory invariants need not imply microscopic gapped homotopy outside the controlled classification setting.
- An EFT classification does not retain all lattice-scale information.

## Paper 5: Realizability of bordism and homotopy classes

### Microscopic phase and invertibility

A microscopic object consists of a local or uniformly almost-local lattice interaction, symmetry data, a ground-state convention, and a gap uniform in system size. A microscopic phase is a stable smooth or gapped homotopy class after adding atomic product-state ancillas.

An object is invertible if there is another system such that their stack is gapped-homotopic, after stabilization, to a fixed atomic system. Invertibility is stronger than possessing an additive invariant.

### Freed-Hopkins scope

`ESTABLISHED`: Freed and Hopkins classify deformation classes of reflection-positive invertible extended topological field theories of fixed symmetry type using maps of spectra and bordism data. Their main discrete classification is expressed using a torsion subgroup of a mapping group involving the Madsen-Tillmann spectrum and the Anderson dual.

The application to lattice phases assumes the existence and validity of low-energy effective-field-theory approximations. It is not a theorem that every abstract bordism class has a local gapped lattice representative.

For symmetry type \((H_n,\rho_n)\), with \(\rho_n:H_n\to O_n\) and compact internal kernel, the discrete reflection-positive invertible topological theories are classified by the torsion subgroup

\[
[MTH,\Sigma^{n+1}I\mathbb Z(1)]_{\mathrm{tors}}.
\]

The broader nontorsion extension to all non-topological reflection-positive invertible theories is conjectural in that work. The microscopic interpretation additionally assumes emergent low-energy relativistic behavior, a system-size-independent gap, invertibility, and realization of the counted field-theory class.

### Kubota scope

`ESTABLISHED`: Kubota constructs an Omega-spectrum whose homotopy groups describe smooth homotopy groups of invertible gapped quantum spin systems on Euclidean spaces, using operator algebras, uniformly almost-local interactions, relaxed lattices, and sheaves on smooth manifolds. The construction also has spatial-symmetry variants.

More precisely, the objects include a chosen nondegenerate gapped GNS ground state, uniform almost-locality, smooth parameter families over manifolds, and stabilization by atomic systems. The compact Lie on-site symmetry construction yields the stated naive equivariant variant. It is not automatically a genuine representation-graded equivariant spectrum.

Kubota's spectrum is microscopic, but it is not the condensed spectrum proposed here. It must not be identified with the Freed-Hopkins bordism spectrum or with a condensed spectrum without a comparison theorem.

### Realization matrix

Each candidate class must be assigned one status:

- `REALIZED`: an explicit interaction, symmetry action, and uniform gap verification map to the claimed class.
- `CANDIDATE`: an explicit model is proposed, but one of class computation, symmetry verification, or uniform gap proof is missing.
- `OPEN`: no realization or obstruction theorem is known.
- `OBSTRUCTED`: a theorem rules out realization under the stated microscopic rules.

The phrase "no known model" means `OPEN`, not `OBSTRUCTED`.

Useful realized test cases include:

- the class-D Kitaev Majorana chain for the nontrivial one-dimensional fermionic \(\mathbb Z_2\) class;
- the BDI chain together with the interacting eightfold reduction;
- the spin-1 AKLT chain as a nontrivial \(SO(3)\)-protected one-dimensional bosonic phase.

For each example, Paper 5 must state the interaction, symmetry, boundary convention, gap theorem, microscopic invariant, and comparison to the abstract class. Agreement in examples is evidence for a comparison, not a general equivalence theorem.

### Relative transition charge

For a family over \(B\) with gapless locus \(\Sigma\), a phase invariant on \(U=B\setminus\Sigma\) may have a connecting class

\[
\partial\nu\in E^{q+1}(B,U).
\]

`ESTABLISHED` as algebraic topology: this is the connecting morphism in the long exact sequence of a pair. `PROPOSED` as physics: under a specified comparison map, interpret it as a transition charge supported near \(\Sigma\). A model computation such as the SSH winding jump can test the proposal, but one example does not establish the interacting general case.

Localization to a neighborhood \(N\) of \(\Sigma\) requires the hypotheses of excision. A further identification with \(E^{q+1-c}(\Sigma)\) requires \(\Sigma\) to be a closed codimension-\(c\) submanifold with an \(E\)-oriented normal bundle. Without that orientation, the target is twisted \(E\)-cohomology. The physical-charge interpretation additionally requires a natural microscopic comparison map.

## Synthesis dependency graph

1. Paper 1 defines admissible families and proves uniform propagation control.
2. Paper 2 defines observable C*-algebra families and isolates the exact solid K-theory bridge.
3. Paper 3 defines the uniformly gapped subfunctor and the allowed adiabatic equivalences.
4. Paper 4 constructs comparison maps only in controlled sectors and lists data lost by EFT reduction.
5. Paper 5 studies which invertible classes have microscopic representatives and where realization remains open.
6. The synthesis may construct the stabilized invertible condensed spectrum only conditionally on the compatibility of Steps 1 through 5.

No later paper may use a stronger form of an earlier result than that earlier paper proves.

## Formalization map

### Lean library

The Lean representation should formalize the dependency graph and theorem interfaces, not pretend to prove deep analytic results absent from Mathlib. Planned declarations include:

- status labels and provenance records;
- finite-volume local interactions;
- parameterized uniform-bound predicates;
- finite-spectrum gap and uniform-gap predicates;
- spectral flattening for finite Hermitian matrices under an explicit gap witness;
- abstract phase equivalence and stacking interfaces;
- a relative-charge interface based on a connecting morphism;
- explicit axioms or imported theorem interfaces for Lieb-Robinson, spectral flow, and realization maps.

Every axiom must be named and documented. Executable examples are not proofs of thermodynamic theorems.

### Haskell demonstrations

Haskell code should test finite models and contract invariants:

- Paper 1: finite metric lattices, interaction tails, and numerical Lieb-Robinson envelopes;
- Paper 2: finite-dimensional involution, positivity, state evaluation, and sup-norm checks;
- Paper 3: finite-size gap sequences and perturbation experiments;
- Paper 4: SSH spectral flattening and winding computations;
- Paper 5: a small realizability registry with verified symmetry and gap witnesses;
- Synthesis: cross-paper dependency and status validator.

The Haskell programs provide executable checks. They do not establish infinite-volume theorems.

## Global nonclaims

- Condensed mathematics does not itself prove locality, positivity, a spectral gap, EFT equivalence, or microscopic realizability.
- A stack or spectrum does not classify general noninvertible topological order without higher excitation and defect data.
- A K-group is not automatically the set of microscopic phases.
- Pointwise gapped families are not automatically uniformly gapped.
- Bordism classification of invertible TFTs is not automatically a lattice classification.
- A proposed comparison map is not an equivalence until its domain, codomain, continuity, symmetry, and homotopy properties are proved.
